Reading Management

December 24 2018

Bookmarking links and figuring out what is the most quality use of my time in terms of reading has long been an interesting conundrum to me. I've written about it before and more or less follow that system I talked about a year ago.

However, there are some changes to this system and I'd like to go into a little more detail about the apps I'm using. This guide was also inspired by a discussion I had on Twitter a few days ago around personal blogging and social media. The year long backlash (techlash) against Facebook has led to a strong chorus of folks calling for you to delete your Facebook account. But what do we replace it with? And by extension, how do we find things to read online?

I like to keep things simple. I like being recommended some sort of article about a topic I'm interested in (sport, tech) and then reading it with minimal advertising and distraction.

Everything starts with Twitter for me. I spend probably a little too much time on the platform but it's so just such a nice way to find information in my areas of interest. If someone I follow and trust has linked an article it's probably going to be good. It's simple word of mouth.

This isn't enough though. What if it's a long read and I simply don't have the time to get through the whole piece. This is where bookmarks come in. I use Pinboard which is perhaps the most simple yet elegant bookmarking service around. It has an API and as such there are heaps of different native apps that work beautifully with my different devices. I'm a iOS guy (iPad, iPhone) and do a tonne of reading on the iPad so having a nice bookmarking app on it is important to me.

I'm currently using Pinner which adheres to the standards I'm looking for. The design is clean and it doesn't feel out of place on iOS, it has as an option to add a new bookmark from the share sheet, it enables quick and easy editing of things like titles and supports Safari's show reader view which gets rid of obtrusive ads.

I like keeping all of my bookmarks uniform and make sure all titles are lower case, so the editing feature is very important to me (when you add a bookmark from the share sheet it's usually all uppercase automatically). This is weird I know; I think I do this because I love the structure of programming languages that have a case convention.

Twitter is great and I find lot's of good things there but what if I don't want to spend as much time on Twitter. This is a problem that RSS solves. The article I wrote last year goes into more detail about RSS but to cut to the chase, I still believe that RSS is a very underutilized technology. Maybe because it tends to be a little finicky to get it up and running? To actually add things to a feed and then how to manage the clutter is not easy.

I think I understand RSS because I've used podcasting clients (my favorite is Overcast) for quite a while.

I have a select few writers that when they post something new I want it straight away. This includes Daring Fireball, M.G. Siegler and Stratechery. I want immediate access to the linksto their articles.

Now I could use some sort of RSS reader to do this job. But that requires me getting a new app that I'd then have to manage. I don't want to do that. I want to use something that exists on all my devices. Something that I already manage. The answer is email. The humble email.

The service that connects RSS to email for me is Blogtrottr. It provides a service that simply works; by no means is it elegant (the web app is trash) but when you understand what the limitations are you're good to go. I get a daily digest email if any of my RSS subscriptions are updated; if I don't have time to read the content at that exact moment I'll chuck the link into Pinboard.

Blogtrottr isn't just for traditional media. It can also be used for say a YouTube channel (get an email everytime Marques Brownlee posts a new video) or Twitter account. This is the flexibility of RSS. This is why open standards like RSS are always better at the end of the day for the customer.

Finally I get things to read through email subscriptions. For just over a year now I've been a happy customer of The Sizzle where I get the latest in tech news (from an Aussies perspective) as well as some more obscure stuff (like this 1984 Macintosh manual that was posted about a week ago). It's refreshing to have tech news written by an Aussie as most of what's online can be way too America centric. I also look forward to to receiving the Hacker Newsletter every Saturday morning as this always has an array of the best tech content of the week. I highly recommend it!

Not all of what I've mentioned above is free but it's hopefully very affordable for most. I pay $11 a year for Pinboard which I think is a brilliant deal. Pinboard is a service I'll continue using for the long term. I pay Blogtrotter $5 a month for their Lite plan. As I said above, it has some flaws but it works for me.

I'd also like to include (build) a few things into this reading system in the future. I still lack a great macOS experience for Pinboard. I tried Spillo but it just wasn't for me. I'm thinking of trying to build something from scratch using the Pinboard API and React. It might also be a nice way to learn how to use Electron so I can have a native Mac app instead of something just on the web. I'm also still not really satisfied with Blogtrotter. I think I could build an RSS to email service myself (maybe using Active Mailer but I'd need some guidance on where to start). The frustration is really based on the crappy Blogtrotter web app; the back-end part is rock solid.
